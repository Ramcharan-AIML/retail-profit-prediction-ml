🗺️ Next Step: Phase 2 - The Art of Scaling
Now we are ready for the step you started in your notebook: Scaling.

Why do we do this? Look at our X_train data. Sales can be in the thousands (e.g., $5,000), while Quantity is usually a small single digit (like 2 or 5). If we leave the numbers like this, the model might think Sales is a thousand times more "important" than Quantity just because the numbers are bigger. Scaling puts all continuous numerical features on a level playing field, usually centered around 0.

The Golden Rules of Scaling:

Never scale Dummy Variables: We only scale continuous numbers. We leave our one-hot encoded columns (the True/False or 1/0 columns) exactly as they are.
Beware of Data Leakage: We must fit our scaler (learn the mean and standard deviation) on the Training Data ONLY. Then, we use those learned numbers to transform both the Training and Testing data. If we fitted the scaler on the whole dataset, information from the test set would "leak" into the training process, giving us fake confidence in our model.

The Real-World Guide to Underfitting vs. Overfitting
Right now, you saw that our model did poorly on the Test Data. But why did it do poorly? There are two main diseases a model can have:

1. Underfitting (The "Lazy Student")

What it is: The model is too simple or didn't learn enough. It didn't find the underlying pattern in the data.
Real-world analogy: Imagine a student who studies for a math test by only reading the chapter titles. They will score terribly on the practice test and terribly on the final exam.
How we spot it: The model's error is very high on the Training Data AND very high on the Testing Data.
2. Overfitting (The "Memorizer")

What it is: The model is too complex. Instead of learning the pattern, it literally memorized the exact numbers in the training data, including all the random noise and weird outliers.
Real-world analogy: Imagine a student who memorizes the exact answers to the practice test (Question 1 is A, Question 2 is C). They score 100% on the practice test. But on the real final exam, the numbers in the questions are slightly changed, and the student fails miserably.
How we spot it: The model's error is super low (perfect) on the Training Data, but immediately falls apart and gets high error on the Testing Data.
So, what disease does our model have? We can't tell just by looking at 5 test predictions! We know it did poorly on the "final exam" (Test data), but we need to know how it did on the "practice test" (Training data) to diagnose the problem.

🗺️ Next Step: Phase 4 - Model Diagnostics (Metrics)
We need a way to mathematically score our model rather than just eyeballing 5 numbers. We will use three standard metrics:

MAE (Mean Absolute Error): On average, how many dollars is our prediction off by? (Lower is better).
RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error): Similar to MAE, but it heavily penalizes huge mistakes (like being off by $1000). (Lower is better).
R² (R-Squared): The "Accuracy" score for regression. 1.0 is perfect, 0 means it's just guessing the average, and negative means it's worse than guessing.
💻 Your Turn: The Diagnosis
Create a cell, paste this, and run it. This will show us our scores on the Practice Test (Train) vs the Final Exam (Test).

import numpy as np
from sklearn.metrics import mean_absolute_error, mean_squared_error, r2_score
# 1. Ask the model to predict on the TRAINING data (the practice test)
y_pred_train = lr_model.predict(X_train)
# --- Calculating Metrics ---
def print_metrics(actual, predicted, dataset_name):
    mae = mean_absolute_error(actual, predicted)
    rmse = np.sqrt(mean_squared_error(actual, predicted))
    r2 = r2_score(actual, predicted)
    
    print(f"--- {dataset_name} Performance ---")
    print(f"MAE (Off by roughly): ${mae:.2f}")
    print(f"RMSE (Penalty for huge errors): ${rmse:.2f}")
    print(f"R-Squared Score: {r2:.2f}\n")
# Print Training vs Testing
print_metrics(y_train, y_pred_train, "TRAINING DATA (Practice Test)")

🗺️ Next Step: Phase 5 - Controlling the Beast (Regularization)
Our Baseline Standard Linear Regression model failed. It has too much freedom to make crazy guesses based on 48 features.

We need to put the puppy on a leash. This mathematical leash is called Regularization. It literally penalizes the model for having excessively large coefficients (weights).

We will try two leashes:

Ridge Regression (L2 Penalty): It shrinks all feature weights down closer to zero. It says to the model, "You can use all 48 features, but you aren't allowed to rely too heavily on any single one."
Lasso Regression (L1 Penalty): It is much harsher. It also shrinks weights to zero, but it actually forces some weights to become exactly zero. It says to the model, "Half of these 48 features are garbage. I'm deleting them. You can only use the most important ones."

What does this catastrophic failure tell us? It tells us that our problem wasn't just "overfitting." The problem is our foundational assumption. Linear Regression strictly assumes that profit follows a neat, straight-line relationship: $y = mx + b$.

But think about retail in the real world: Profit isn't linear; it's conditional.

If a customer buys a $1000 computer, and the discount is > 30%, then we take a massive loss.

A Linear Regression model fails spectacularly trying to draw a straight line through those complex "If/Then" relationships. Additionally, those massive extreme outliers we saw in Phase 1 (profits of +$8000 and losses of -$6000) are totally breaking the linear math curve.

How Do We Win This? The Pivot ⚔️
If our data is basically a bunch of complex "If/Then" rules, we should use a model designed to learn "If/Then" rules.

Enter the Random Forest.

Instead of trying to draw one perfectly straight line, a Random Forest builds an army of hundreds of "Decision Trees". Each tree asks a series of yes/no questions (e.g., Is Discount > 20%? Is Sales > $50?). It averages the guesses of all the trees together. It naturally handles extreme outliers and captures non-linear relationships effortlessly.

Yes, exactly! It is 100% automatic.

To understand how it's automatic, think about the simple math of how any Linear Regression model makes a prediction.

The formula for predicting Profit looks like this: Predicted Profit = (Weight_1 * Sales) + (Weight_2 * Quantity) + (Weight_3 * Discount) ...

During training, Lasso looks at a feature (let's pretend it's the state of Wyoming) and realizes, "This feature is just adding noise, it doesn't help me predict profit at all." Because of the "L1 Penalty" math built into Lasso, it takes the weight for Wyoming and forces it down to exactly 0.0.

So, when predicting future data, the math looks like this: ... + (0.0 * Wyoming) + ...

Because anything multiplied by zero is zero, that feature literally vanishes from the mathematical equation! It is permanently ignored for all future predictions.

This is why Lasso is extremely popular in the medical field (e.g., if you have data on 10,000 human genes, Lasso will automatically delete the 9,900 genes that don't cause the disease, leaving you with only the important ones).
